Project 4. Algorithms development for Philips smart mask

Design Background

Due to the air pollution and pandemic situation, masks become closely interact with people. It is rational to create a solution that delivers respiratory care based on mask. Philips active mask identified several directions to deliver respiratory care including:
1. stress relief for daily commuters;
2. breathing technique practice for meditation or yoga beginner;
3. symptom management for asthma suffers;
4. Lamaze breathing technique practice for natural birth;
5. respiratory health monitor for wearable users.
Active mask with an electric fan would be an excellent platform to achieve this solution since the fan itself could be used to collect breathing patterns. Together with other sensors, online databases, more health benefits can be created to consumers.

Alogrithms

To realize identified health benefits, algorithms need to be developed and validated. Fan signal can be used to extract breathing related parameters. With this input, together with other sensors and database, a series of algorithms that target respiratory rate, volume, auto adaption, estimation of inhaled CO2 etc can be created. A summary of the potential algorithm framework is shown in the Fig 4.2. Some of them can be readily realized either one firmware or app, while some others need further investigations.

Fig 4.2 algorithm framework

Fig 4.3 respiratory rate algorithm

respiratory rate algorithm
From the collected breathing patterns, there are steps to realize respiratory rate algorithm.
Step 1. collect real-time fan signal data from fan module.
Step 2. apply a smoothing filter (weighted average) to remove the fluctuation of fan signals and enhance signal to noise ratio.
Step 3. transform waveform from semi-sine wave to square wave, which needs determine threshold by moving average. For data above this threshold, a logic “1” is set, while “0” for others. After the square wave is transformed, the time that denotes one breathing cycle can be acquired.
Step 4. calculate respiratory rate in a sampling window (e.g., 15 s sampling windows), followed by expansion to 1-minute scale, since the respiratory rate is in breaths per minute unit.

Fig 4.4 inhale-exhale ratio alogrithm

inhale-exhale ratio algorithm
I:E ratio is also retrieved from the breathing patterns. The accuracy of I:E ratio significantly relies on fan response speed to external airflow and method to extract the baseline of fan signal. To acquire I:E ratio, the determination of the transforming point from inhalation-to-exhalation and exhalation-to-inhalation is important. By investigating the differential pressure sensor (DP) data in the Figure, steps are taken to deduce I:E ratio:
Step 1. detect local peaks from first-order derivative of the breath waveform.
Step 2. estimate the inspiration duration as the time difference between a peak to its preceding valley.
Step 3. estimate the expiration duration as the time difference between the valley and its preceding peak.
Step 4. define I/E ratio as the ratio that the inspiration duration over expiration duration.

Fig 4.5 inhaled CO2 concentration algorithm

inhaled CO2 concentration algorithm
The smart mask has not integrate CO2 sensor in hardware due to no small-sized and ultrafast sensor available. The inhaled CO2 algorithm is achieved by numerical calculations. Mask Simulation Tool is developed for When given parameters of fan, filter, leakage, breathing, and environment, this tool can calculate multiple factors such as inner temperature, humidity, flow rates, and pressure, and inhaled CO2 level, etc.

breathing volume algorithm
To conduct breathing volume algorithm, the relationship between fan response (fan speed change) and breathing patterns (different respiratory rates and tidal volumes) is investigated. Typical results are depicted in fig(a). The fan speed change increases with the elevated tidal volumes under one breathing rates. This phenomenon exhibits the feasibility to deduce breathing volume according to fan response. The relationship of fan speed change with tidal volume depends on specific respiratory rate. This relationship is not readily to use since the regression equation should be deduced for each respiratory rate. The relationship between ventilation volume (tidal volume * respiratory rate) and fan speed change is further looked into. One clear regression model was discovered regardless the respiratory rate, as illustrated in fig(b). This means if a certain airflow (such as 12 L/min), no matter how this airflow is generated (e.g., either by 12 brpm/1L tidal volume, or by 20 brpm/0.6 L tidal volume) was introduced to the mask, the fan response would be the same (e.g., around 300 rpm for L1). The reasons behind this phenomenon is yet unclear and suppose to rely on the design of fan. When a fan possesses much higher torque than the others, making it more difficult to change under the same airflow. With a simple transposing process, the relationship between ventilation volume and fan speed change can be deduced, as shown in the lower part of fig(c). This relationship is useful in practice since the fan speed change as input can be acquired. The regression equations for L1, L2 and L3 are listed in the figure. When the ventilation volume is calculated, the tidal volume can be readily calculated by: tidal volume=ventilation volume/respiratory rate.
